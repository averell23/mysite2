misconceptions about copyrights

There are still many misconceptions about copyrights and trademarks and how they apply to the Internet. I pick some of the most popular to give a quick overview of the topic.

If it's on the web it's free to use

No matter if the material is on the Internet, in a museum or a book: You must always obtain the author's permission to re-publish his work. (It's usually o.k. to make copies for private use, but private use does not include using it for your own pages.)

I can use any material if my page is not commercial

It doesn't matter if your page is "commercial" or not. If you publish someone else's work you always need the author's permission.

I need permission to link to other pages and must not "deep link"

This isn't really a copyright issue, but at least in Germany you may "deep link" to your heart's desire. The German supreme court (Bundesgerichtshof) decided that as long as a page is publicly available on the net you may link to it, whether the author likes it or not.

I must not use a trademark without the owner's permission

While you must not use someone's trademark if it is your own (e.g. to endorse the sale of your product), you may of course use any trademark for describing the product. (e.g. when you write about Microsoft Word, Microsoft cannot stop you from calling their product "Microsoft Word").

In the European Union you also have the right to use a trademark to declare compatibility ("This program runs under Windows XP").

You also don't need disclaimers or other vodoo like "(tm)" signs (at least not in Germany).

If I change something, I own it.

By changing an existing work you create a new one. That doesn't void the rights of the original author, however. You still need his permission to distribute the new work

Disclaimers

This is also no copyright question. However, it has become a widespread practice to use "Disclaimers" to avoid legal troubles. These things usually fulfil the same function as magical incantations and are equally reliable. Almost all of them have no legal value whatsoever. (Again, this is true for Germany).

Simply put: An illegal act doesn't become legal by you willing (or disclaiming) it so.

Copyrights protect ideas

A copyright doesn't protect an idea, but only the expression of an idea. The idea itself (e.g. "a book about people falling in love") can not be protected.
